Puratan Shastar Vidiya

As of 2025, it is the 180th barsi of the start of the Anglo-Sikh Wars - due to betrayals, circumstances out of our control, these wars ended in defeat 1849.
Our sovereignty dismantled, our institutions hijacked. Loss of institutions, came loss of agency, loss of direction, loss of identity, and loss of destiny.
Shastar Vidiya driven underground. Gatka became largely performance, and Nihangta became largely ceremonial.
Singh Khalsa spirit of independence, was suppressed. Sikhs have been in a recovery period ever since.
Gur Kirpa Nal, a few Gur Piyareh Die Hards carried the torch. We have this opportunity, not to be the messiah, but to do a small part to help fan the embers
This Akhara stands as a humble picking up the baton, that the mainstream dropped, after the battle of Sabroan 1846.
We are not the messiah, but we are those who remember, and wish to spend our humble resources in making sure this baton, does not get dropped and forgotten again. So that our future generations will not grow up weak, divided, or directionless.

Not for revenge - but for alignment to who we are.

This is a foundation sttone, for us to give the future generations hope and steel self esteem again. A grass roots initiative, instilling cultural and religious pride in our community.
This ਅਣਖ gives an identity of strength, and through strength one attains truth, from weakness stems crookedness and lowliness.
And the 180 years of defeat, we are in fact testimony to our strength, as only the strong, can bear defeat

Why is our Akhara different

Many spaces talk about technique.
We train to apply it.

At our Akhara, the priority is simple: Can you actually perform the movement under pressure?

We keep theory concise and relevant, then move quickly into:

  • Repetition and drilling - building reflex, timing, control and the 6th sense needed when in tunnel vision
  • Pressure testing - controlled sparring to see what truly works. There is a heavy focus on the psychological realities of self defense and traditional techniques
  • Functional strength - training the body to support the technique, most Akhareh have the false notion "techniques beats all", whereas we follow the maryada laid out by Guru Angad Dev and Guru Hargobind of valuing physical culture.
  • Consistency - showing up, putting in the rounds, improving week by week

There's no ego and no performance for show. We follow the Khalsa maxim of "Aap Da Bhala, Sarbat Da Bhala" - that ones own wellbeing lies in the wellbeing of everyone else.

Just honest training, safe but realistic practice, and a focus on skill that holds up when it matters. Khalsa fighting techniques are supreme, and can stand under the toughest of circumstances.

We respect tradition - but we honour it by making the techniques live, not relegation them to text book try demos.
If you're looking for an environment built on application tradition, realism and development, you're welcome in the Akhara.

Train Hard. Stay Humble. Improve Together.

The Grandmaster

"He was a man of great aggression. [As] soon as he picked up a gatka stick he grit his teeth and his eyes went blood shot. If he wanted to kill you hel could just do like that."
Baba Ram Singh, Jathedar of Akali Phula Singh Burj on Baba Gian Singh "Sutantar"

"My Ustad was such you could not look him in the eye. Such was his Robh (intimidating power)"
Baba Mohinder Singh Katapa (give of beatings), one of the most skilled and brutal swordsman of the 20th century, speaking about how his own teacher, Baba Gian Singh "Sutantar" (pictured) was the roughest

In his youth, Baba Gian Singh would lift weigths and was very strong. His reputation was such, even in old age no one would try their luck with this old master of war. Physical training, gives one a Strength that cannot be quantified, a confidence and aura. Everyone would address his as "Rabh Ji" as a sign of the highest respect.
